This is the first post of the Nobel Laureates Impressions series. Please check here to find out more posts in the series.

When I decided to write this series, I didn't think for a second to put Chen-Ning Yang in the first post for a few reasons. First, he probably got the Nobel prize (in 1957) earlier than anyone alive (except, of course, Tsung-Dao Lee, who shared the Nobel prize with CN Yang). Indeed, he was among the youngest to got the prize (at 35, only 16 laureates were younger at the time of award) and he is 93 years old already. Second, he was the first Chinese Nobel laureate (again, same for Tsung-Dao Lee). Third, CN Yang has made the biggest contributions to physics among all Chinese. He is considered the "best" Chinese physicist of all time. Of course, the last reason is that I have met him in person, but not Tsung-Dao Lee, which is the criteria of this post series.

NTU has a CN Yang Scholarship program, named in honour of CN Yang. Among other benefits, CN Yang scholars have the opportunity to interact with CN Yang privately when he visits Singapore. It is because of this scholarship program, CN Yang comes once every one to two years to Singapore.

I have long been a fan of CN Yang. Since primary school, I got to know that he is the first Chinese to be awarded Nobel prize. But not until I read his biography, did I realise how important he is to modern physics. There is a good summary of his works (13 problems in 4 fields) by Fudan University professor Shi Yu, published on a Chinese journal Physics. I quote it here:

(A) Statistical Mechanics

A1. 1952 Phase Transition

A2. 1957 Bosons

A3. 1967 Yang—Baxter Equation

A4. 1969 Finite Temperature

(B) Condensed Matter Physics

B1. 1961 Flux Quantization

B2. 1962 ODLRO

(C) Particle Physics

C1. 1956 Parity Nonconservation

C2. 1957 T, C and P

C3. 1960 Neutrino Experiment

C4. 1964 CP Nonconservation

(D) Field Theory

D1. 1954 Gauge Theory

D2. 1974 Integral Formalism

D3. 1975 Fibre Bundle

The contributions were also engraved in the black marble cube given to CN Yang by Tsinghua University in his 90th birthday.

However, despite the fact that he is one of the most important physicist alive, most Chinese got to know him because of his young wife, not because of his contributions to physics. If you search Yang's name in Chinese, the top results are almost exclusively about his wife. 老牛吃嫩草 (lao niu chi nen cao, old cow eats young grass) is the world they use to describe Yang's second marriage.  They can't believe that a young and beautiful girl can fall in love with an old man more than 80 years old. "She loves the fame and money of Yang only, not Yang himself." they think.

Many Chinese also complained that Yang didn't come back to "serve" the country when the country needed him the most and only came back after 2000 when China already became one of the strongest economy in the world. There are also numerous articles talking about this. Yang is often compared with Qian Xuesen, who came back to China to develop rocket and missiles after becoming a professor in the US, and Deng Jiaxian, who was a childhood friend of Yang and also went to US to study and later came back to China to develop China's first atomic bomb. "Science has no boundaries, but scientists have nationality" (科学无国界，可是科学家有国籍) is topic that worth to write a post itself. I am not trying to argue about it here.

Let's come back to the meat of the post: my personal impression of CN Yang. I met him at OCPA8 conference in 2014 which changed my research direction to physics.
